# Say It Right — Slide Outline

A 14-slide training activity on cross-cultural greetings and inclusive communication, for MQBS2010 Assessment 3.

**Skill:** Cross-cultural greeting / pronunciation as inclusive communication
**DEI lens:** Developing inclusive communication (with global citizenship moments)
**Target audience:** Tutorial peers (Macquarie Business School)
**Runtime:** 12 minutes facilitated + 3 minutes debrief = 15-minute showcase slot
**Style:** Warm, organic — orange and cream palette, illustrated diverse people, soft serif headlines

---

## Slide 1 — Say It Right (title)

- Title: **Say It Right**
- Subtitle: *Small greetings, big respect.*
- Team names underneath
- Warm orange + cream palette
- One illustrated image of diverse people greeting each other
- One short tagline so first-time viewers immediately know what the deck teaches

---

## Slide 2 — The Moment

Opening hook — one short anecdote that sets the emotional stakes.

- A 30-second story: a moment when a name was mispronounced, a greeting was missed, or a small effort changed how someone felt
- Quote-style layout: big type, lots of white space, one supporting image
- Speaker brief: keep it personal, keep it short — under 40 seconds spoken
- Goal: make the audience *feel* the topic before any content arrives

---

## Slide 3 — Why This Matters

One stat, one sentence — make the case quickly.

- Lead stat: research on mispronounced names and workplace belonging (cite source on slide)
- One supporting sentence in plain language — no bullets
- Visual: a single strong image, not a chart
- Avoid: walls of text, multiple stats, lecturing tone

---

## Slide 4 — The Universal Rule

The framing that lowers the room's anxiety about the practice that's coming.

- Big centred phrase: **Effort > Perfection**
- Subtitle: *You don't need to be fluent — you need to try.*
- Single slide, single idea, lots of breathing room
- This frames every greeting slide that follows

---

## Slide 5 — Mandarin · 你好 Nǐ hǎo

First practice greeting.

- Native script: 你好
- Phonetic: *NEE-how* (with tone marks)
- Meaning: hello
- Time-of-day variants:
  - 早上好 *Zǎoshang hǎo* — morning
  - 下午好 *Xiàwǔ hǎo* — afternoon
  - 晚上好 *Wǎnshang hǎo* — evening
- Gesture: light nod or handshake — no bow
- Video placeholder: 10-second clip of a native speaker
- Consistent on-screen position for script / phonetic / meaning across all five greeting slides

---

## Slide 6 — Thai · สวัสดี Sawasdee

Second practice greeting — gesture is the lesson here.

- Native script: สวัสดี
- Phonetic: *sa-wat-DEE*
- Time-of-day add-on: สวัสดีตอนเช้า (morning)
- **Gesture: the wai** — hands pressed together at chest, slight head bow
- Cultural note: higher hands = more respect (to elders, monks, teachers)
- Video placeholder: clip of the wai in use
- Illustration of the gesture in the same style as the other greeting slides

---

## Slide 7 — Japanese · こんにちは Konnichiwa

Third practice greeting — bow depths are the teaching moment.

- Native script: こんにちは
- Phonetic: *kon-NEE-chee-wah*
- Time-of-day variants:
  - おはよう *Ohayō* — morning, casual
  - おはようございます — morning, formal
  - こんばんは *Konbanwa* — evening
- **Gesture: bow depth diagram** — 15° casual, 30° respectful, 45° deeply respectful
- Cultural note: eye contact during the bow varies by context
- Video placeholder

---

## Slide 8 — Hindi · नमस्ते Namaste

Fourth practice greeting — meaning behind the gesture.

- Native script: नमस्ते
- Phonetic: *nuh-muh-STAY*
- Time-of-day variants:
  - सुप्रभात *Suprabhāt* — morning
  - शुभ संध्या *Śubh sandhyā* — evening
- **Gesture: namaste mudra** — palms together at heart, slight head bow
- Meaning of the gesture: *"the divine in me bows to the divine in you"*
- Video placeholder

---

## Slide 9 — Arabic · السلام عليكم As-salāmu ʿalaykum

Fifth practice greeting — includes the reply, a detail that respects the full exchange.

- Native script: السلام عليكم
- Phonetic: *as-sa-LAA-mu a-LAY-kum*
- Meaning: peace be upon you
- Reply: وعليكم السلام *Wa ʿalaykum as-salām* — and peace be upon you
- Time-of-day variants:
  - صباح الخير *Ṣabāḥ al-khayr* — morning
  - مساء الخير *Masāʾ al-khayr* — evening
- **Gesture:** right hand to chest after handshake — sincerity and warmth
- Right hand only (avoid left)
- Video placeholder

---

## Slide 10 — Yaama · On Country

The slide that replaces the removed German one. **This is the deck's biggest differentiator at Macquarie.**

- Open with a written Acknowledgement of the Wallumattagal people of the Dharug Nation — Traditional Custodians of the land Macquarie sits on
- Teach one local Aboriginal greeting:
  - **Yaama** (Gamilaraay — northern NSW)
  - **Warami** (Dharug — Macquarie's local language)
- Check with [Walanga Muru / Indigenous Strategy office](https://www.mq.edu.au/about/indigenous-strategy) for the right word and protocol before submission
- Frame: *"the most local greeting we'll learn today is the one most of us never learn"*
- This slide directly serves the **global citizenship** lens of the assessment rubric
- No video unless approved by a custodian — a quiet, respectful slide is better

---

## Slide 11 — Try It Yourself

The required interactive moment. The brief explicitly asks for peers to *practice* the skill.

- Instruction: **Pair up with the person next to you**
- 30 seconds each — greet your partner with any greeting + gesture from today
- Then switch
- Big visible timer on the slide for the facilitator to run
- Speaker brief: walk the room while peers practice; don't fix mistakes, just smile and encourage
- **This is the slide that earns the marks** — keep it big and unmissable

---

## Slide 12 — The Cost of Getting It Wrong

Two examples — one personal, one organisational. Don't moralise.

- Micro example: a name mispronounced once = a quiet exclusion (short real story or one-line research finding)
- Macro example: a brand or campaign that bombed because of a greeting or pronunciation miss
  - KFC in China ("we'll eat your fingers off")
  - Pepsi in Taiwan ("bring your ancestors back from the dead")
  - HSBC's "Assume Nothing" → "Do Nothing"
  - Pick one well-documented case
- One sentence linking the two: *small acts and big stakes are the same skill*
- Avoid: pity tone, finger-wagging

---

## Slide 13 — Small Efforts Matter

Closing reframe — calls back to The Universal Rule.

- Big phrase: **Small efforts matter**
- One supporting sentence: *the act of trying IS the respect*
- Visual: a single image of diverse hands in different greeting gestures, or a montage of the team's own gesture practice photos
- One takeaway the audience can use tomorrow

---

## Slide 14 — Get in Touch

Contact + thanks. **Cut the working-notes from the current slide 10 before submitting.**

- Team names and Macquarie emails
- Thank-you line
- Optional: QR code linking to a short feedback form (Google Form / Padlet) your team can read after the showcase
- Optional: one-line call to action — *"Try one new greeting this week and tell us how it landed."*
- Keep the same warm palette and illustrated style as the title slide for a closed loop

## Where to find videos (no copyright issues)

- **Wikimedia Commons** — search "[language] pronunciation"
- **YouTube Creative Commons** — filter search results by CC licence
